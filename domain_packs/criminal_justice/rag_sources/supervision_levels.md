# Supervision Levels and Conditions

Illustrative reference material for the SENTINEL assistant on supervision intensity and conditions.

## Supervision levels
Supervision is assigned at one of three levels — Standard, High, or Specialized — proportional to
assessed risk and need. Standard supervision applies to lower-risk individuals and emphasises
periodic check-ins. High supervision adds more frequent contact and monitoring. Specialized
supervision is reserved for cases needing tailored programmes (for example, substance-use or
mental-health caseloads).

## Proportionality
Supervision intensity should match risk and need. Over-supervising low-risk individuals is costly and
can be counter-productive (criminogenic); scarce resources should concentrate on higher-risk cases.
A high risk score is a reason to consider closer support, not a reason for punitive action.

## Conditions and dynamic factors
Conditions should target the dynamic, changeable factors behind a person's risk — unemployment,
substance use, unstable housing — through support and programmes rather than sanctions alone. Static
factors such as age and criminal history cannot be changed and should not drive punitive conditions.

## Review and change
Supervision level is reviewed as circumstances change. Documented improvement — sustained employment,
completed treatment, stable housing — is grounds to step a person down to a lower level, and is a
common, legitimate reason for a case officer to override a model's risk band.
